The Dirichlet problem on bounded domain

The basic problem takes the form of solving for an unknown function \( u:\overline{\Omega} \to \mathbb{R} \) where \( \Omega \) is an open, connected subset of \( \mathbb{R}^n \), the boundary value problem

$$ \begin{equation}\label{eq:PDE} \begin{cases} \begin{aligned} \Delta u &= 0 &&\text{in}\; \Omega, \\ u &= g &&\text{on}\; \partial\Omega \end{aligned} \end{cases} \end{equation} $$

where \( g:\partial \Omega \to \mathbb{R}\) is a given continuous function. The boundary value problem \(\eqref{eq:PDE}\) is extremely important and has a rich history of development, through

  • Calculus of variation (Dirichlet’s principle).
  • Potential theory (Integral representation).
  • Subharmonic function lifting method (Poincare’s approach, or Perron’s method).

The following is quoted from [1]:

Three basic approaches came out of this: Poincaré-type methods which use subharmonic functions, integral equation methods based on potential representations of harmonic functions, and finally, variational methods related to minimizing the Dirichlet energy. In this chapter, we will study the method of subharmonic functions in detail.

Notes

The lecture note [1] is a very nice introduction to the history of the subject. This note outline nicely the historical development of the method for \(\eqref{eq:PDE}\). For a local setting, the so-called Perron’s method can be applied to many situation, including a very general fully nonlinear second-order PDE. The goal of this blog post is to summarize the idea and subtlety of the method in many cases. In particular, the application of the method in the nonlocal setting will be considered.
